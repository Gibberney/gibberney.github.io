France, the Myth of Resistance, and the Stories We Tell

When you think of France during World War II, what's the first story that comes to mind? If you're like a lot of folks, it's probably a tale of brave resistance fighters scheming under cover of darkness, tirelessly working to kick Nazi tail out of their homeland. It’s the stuff of legends—literally. France's national mythology of heroic resistance is a captivating narrative, but like most stories with black-and-white heroes and villains, the reality is a bit more nuanced.

Historians have estimated that the active resistance movement in France was actually quite modest in terms of numbers. The sad truth? A large portion of the population either actively collaborated with the occupying forces or simply kept their heads down and went about their lives as if nothing were amiss. Yet, post-war, the image of a nation standing united against the invaders is a powerful one, providing a narrative that many find easier to digest.

Why Reinvent History?

You have to ask: why would a country lean into a myth that diverges so much from reality? Frankly, it's human nature. When faced with a period in our collective past that we're not too proud of, retelling the story in a way that is a tad more heroic can be a coping mechanism. Yes, the concept of a nation rewriting its history might seem sneaky, but it's kind of like scrolling through your old Facebook photos and skipping over those college party shots. Maybe you didn't need to bring the toga to P.E. 101.

Nations do similar things. After WWII, the French spirit needed a bit of a boost. There was a need to rally around a common identity and establish a narrative of bravery and defiance. It’s understandably preferable to think of oneself as a valiant resistor rather than an indifferent bystander or, worse, a collaborator.

The Role of Memory and Myth

Collective memory isn't just about what happened; it's about what we remember happening. It's like a game of massive, historical "Telephone" where details shift over time to serve present needs. Memory is selective. Often, it just plain forgets the awkward bits, like how to conjugate verbs correctly in high school French class.

For France, the myth of resistance became a cornerstone of post-war identity. The state-sponsored narratives, films, and literature all helped to weave this tale of widespread bravery. It's not that the French resistance wasn't brave or significant—it was crucial and heroic—but it didn’t involve massive numbers resisting in the way that collective memory has sometimes suggested.

Why This Matters

You might be wondering why all this matters now. It’s not like we're going to hop in a time machine and castigate some Vichy collaborators, right? But understanding how nations construct their histories can help us understand ourselves better. It encourages us to question everything, including the popular and comfortable stories we grew up with. And let’s face it, not everything on the internet is true (spoiler alert).

Recognizing this post-war narrative as more myth than reality might just allow modern France, and other nations with similar stories, to reconcile more fully with their pasts. It paves the way for healing and provides a fuller picture of what happened. In turn, this might lead us to ask better questions about our own national narratives. After all, if we can't face history head-on, we might be doomed to misunderstand it forever.

So the next time someone tells you a grand, sweeping story about national character or historical fact, maybe lean in and ask, "But what’s the rest of the story?" You might be surprised at what you find.
