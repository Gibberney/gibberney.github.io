Dancing with Developers: Lessons from a Non-Techie on Imposter Syndrome and Owning My Lane

Let's lay it all out there: I'm about as technical as a ham sandwich. I'm the guy who gets a little excited when my phone connects to Wi-Fi without asking for the password again. And yet, here I am, spending my days knee-deep in SaaS alongside folks who probably build robots in their spare time. It’s like being a golden retriever in a room full of border collies – everyone's smart, engaged, and pulling off advanced tricks while I sit there wagging my tail, proud of fetching a stick.

All this to say, imposter syndrome? I could write a thesis on it – if I had any idea how to write a thesis. When you're the non-technical person in a very technical environment, it feels a bit like you've accidentally waltzed on stage during a ballet performance. But here’s the kicker: over nearly a decade in this field, I've learned not only to survive but to actually carve out a place for myself without constantly apologizing for my non-coder status.

Every Day is "Bring Your Imposter Syndrome to Work" Day

In the SaaS world, acronyms and jargon fly past like an endless ticker tape parade: APIs, UI/UX, MVP, and a smattering of others that sound more like secret plans than regular business terms. When the engineers start discussing sprints or debating over the best way to refactor code, there are times I just nod along like I understand, hoping I don't look like a deer caught in the headlights. Spoiler alert: I probably do.

But here's what I've learned about imposter syndrome: it's universal. I had a revelation when a seasoned developer confessed how intimidated they felt about user interactions. It dawned on me that we're all feeling like frauds in some area of our work. Somehow, knowing that made it a little easier to swallow. Besides, if I'm not confused at least once a day, I'm probably not learning anything new, right?

Celebrating the Small Wins

While I may not be wiring up cutting-edge software, there are small victories that I’ve learned to celebrate. Like explaining to an engineer how our work impacts the end user in real terms, seeing that light bulb go off, or when I connect the perfect analogy to break down a complex concept. It's these moments that make me feel like I'm contributing in a meaningful way.

There was a memorable moment during a team meeting when I raised my hand and asked, "Okay, but how does this affect Jane from Accounting?" It was as if I had dropped the mic. Suddenly, everyone was speaking a common language – business value – and I realized that there's a place for non-techies in tech, even if it's just to ask the simple questions.

Stop Apologizing, Start Contributing

For the longest time, I felt the need to prefix any tech-heavy discussion with, "I'm not a coder, but..." almost as if apologizing for my existence. But there came a turning point when I realized it was okay – even necessary – to stop apologizing for what I'm not and to start embracing what I am. I'm the guy who takes complex tech solutions and translates them into value for the customer or insights for the team. There's value in being the bridge between the technical and the practical. It's not just important; it's crucial.

So if you're also a non-techie wading through a river of code, remember this: while it might feel like you’re letting the team down with your lack of coding prowess, your unique perspective could be just what’s needed to propel your team forward. It’s about shifting the narrative from what you can't do to what you can offer.

In closing, standing in a room filled with brilliant engineers every day is a bit like perpetual seasoning by fire. But instead of getting burned, I've learned how to give myself a break, celebrate the small wins, and stop letting imposter syndrome keep me in apology mode. Remember, you don’t have to speak C++ to be a key player in the tech world – sometimes, all it takes is asking who it’s all for and why it matters.
